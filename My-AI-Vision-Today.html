01

Main idea

My view is simple: AI is a tool that can dramatically expand what a person can do, but only when it is used consciously.

The main mistake is expecting AI to deliver a finished correct solution. In my view, AI works best when the person holds the direction, provides context, and verifies the result.

The problem starts when a person treats AI like a ready-made mind that should guess the goal, context, constraints, and desired outcome on its own. Opening any AI tool does not mean you have started working with AI correctly. There is a big difference between getting an answer and bringing the work to a real result.

AI should be a copilot, not an autopilot. The human sets the direction, checks the answer, makes the decision, and owns the result.

A strong AI user is not someone who knows a few beautiful prompts. It is someone who can move toward a goal, avoid getting stuck, clarify context, look for alternatives, test assumptions, and build their own working system.

02

The right mindset

Many people either overestimate AI or underestimate it. Some blindly trust the first answer. Others get a weak result and decide AI does not work. Both approaches are shallow.

In the AI world, the winner is not a passive user, but a person who actively searches for solutions. They ask questions, try things, check, compare options, and look for weak points in an idea while it is still easy to change.

When problems appear during the work, the first reaction is often to erase everything and start again. But starting from zero does not mean making it better. Very often, a person simply carries the same mistakes into a new version or creates different ones.

It is better to first understand what exactly does not work, fix that specific place, and move forward. If something needs to be rebuilt, it is better to rebuild it in small parts instead of tearing everything down to zero.

A separate problem is when the mind already lives at a professional level, but real experience does not yet match it (overestimating your own level). A person knows the terminology, reads books, listens to strong people, and starts feeling that they are already far ahead.

But understanding someone else's thoughts is not the same as having your own mastery. It is like football: watching matches, listening to analysis, and explaining who made which mistake is much easier than stepping onto the field and playing at that level yourself. AI does not remove this difference: it helps you learn faster, but it cannot do the practice for you.

You need to be smart enough to start and humble enough to keep learning.

For experienced people, AI can be hard to accept not because their knowledge is bad, but because they spent years working in a certain way, and that way really used to be strong. But AI changes the logic of work: some tasks can now be done faster, differently, and with a lower barrier to entry.

An experienced specialist still has depth, experience, risk prediction, and an understanding of details, but a beginner with the right mindset and strong AI usage can quickly do things that used to be out of reach. The difference in experience does not disappear, but the rules of the game change.

03

AI + domain expertise

AI by itself does not make a person a master. It expands what is already there: thinking, experience, goals, understanding of the field, the ability to ask questions, and the ability to verify answers.

If a person does not understand the topic, they ask general questions and get general answers. If a person knows the field, they see details, formulate more precisely, notice mistakes faster, and get a different level of result.

AI working skill

Understanding models, context, prompts, planning, decomposition, verification, alternatives, risks, and different tools.

Domain knowledge

Real domain knowledge: terminology, details, constraints, quality, typical mistakes, and criteria for a good result.

AI can help you learn a field from zero. But it does not remove the need to read, practice, care about details, and become a specialist.

The real strength is not simply "knowing how to use AI". The real strength appears when AI is combined with experience in a specific field.
04

What it means to learn AI

Learning AI is not about finding one perfect prompt. It is about building a skill: understanding how a model works, how to talk to it, when to give more context, when to ask for a plan, when to break the task down, and when to stop for verification.

Different models work differently. One is better at planning, another at writing code, another at explaining, another at exploring options. If you use every model the same way, it is easy to make the wrong conclusion that AI is weak, when the real problem is the way you are using it.

"Feeling AI" means seeing what is needed right now: more context, smaller steps, another model, clarifying questions, critique of the plan, alternatives, or a practical test.

05

Levels of development with AI

Level
0

Does not understand or got disappointed

The person has not used AI or got a weak result and decided AI is bad. They do not yet see the role of context, models, dialogue, and verification.

Level
1

Starts using AI

The person already asks questions and requests explanations, ideas, texts, and plans. But they often formulate too generally and expect a perfect answer immediately.

Level
2

Understands context

The person sees that answer quality depends on the goal, situation, constraints, examples, previous decisions, and desired format.

Level
3

Can move toward a goal

This is the first solid level. The person does not get stuck because they know how to ask AI how to move forward, how to break down the task, what to verify, and where the risks are.

Level
4

Works systematically

The person uses AI for work, learning, analysis, and decisions. They keep context, a plan, goals, or other notes so they do not start from zero every time.

Level
5

Configures AI for themselves

The person has personal configuration, templates, skills, agents, and workflows. They build their own AI working environment.

Level
6

AI-oriented specialist

The person understands what AI can do today, follows updates, tests new models and tools, chooses the right tool for the task, and gets real results.

06

Practical working system

AI works better when a person does not start every conversation from zero. For that, you need a simple system that keeps context and holds the direction.

Context

The goal, situation, constraints, desired result, what has already been tried, and what does not fit.

Plan

The skeleton of the work: where we are going, the main stages, the current stage, and the next step.

Goals

What a completed stage means, what result must be achieved, and when it is time to move forward.

Personal configuration

Rules you do not want to repeat: language, style, format, typical AI mistakes, and answer requirements.

Skills

Instructions for repeated actions: planning, learning, research, verification, idea checking, productivity.

Agents

Assistants with roles: one searches for data, another analyzes, a third executes, and a fourth checks.

07

Verification and mistakes

AI can give an answer that sounds convincing. That does not mean it is correct. The most dangerous moment is when the answer matches what the person already wanted to hear.

One important skill is not being afraid to ask AI follow-up questions. If the answer is unclear, do not pretend that everything is clear. Ask what it means, why it works this way, what options exist, and what you may not be seeing. This helps you get a better answer, understand the topic more deeply, and build a clearer plan, goals, and next steps.

  • If something is unclear: "Explain this in simple words. What does this point mean? Why are you suggesting it?"
  • If you do not know how to move forward: "Ask me questions that will help you understand the task better."
  • If you want to check the context: "Explain in simple words what you understood from my request so I can check whether you understood me correctly."
  • Ask not for one option, but for 3 alternatives with pros, cons, and risks.
  • Even a good option should be tested with the question: "why might this fail?"
  • If the answer is bad, do not stop: add details, rephrase, break down the task, or change the model.
Do not wait for the perfect first prompt. Learn to improve the conversation until AI starts solving the right task.

Typical mistakes of AI and people

Human mistakes

  • Blind trust in the first answer.
  • Laziness in thinking and checking.
  • Too little context.
  • No plan and no goals.
  • No alternatives.
  • Expecting AI to guess what stayed only in the person's head.

AI mistakes

  • Misunderstands the context.
  • Optimizes for the wrong task.
  • Presents a weak answer with confidence.
  • Agrees with a bad idea.
  • Does extra work when rules are unclear.
  • Gives generic answers when details are missing.
08

Context examples

One of the most common problems: AI can answer logically while solving the wrong task.

Weak understanding

A person is 100 meters away from a car wash. There is traffic on the road. They want to wash the car and ask what is better: walk there or drive there.

AI may say: "walk there", because it is faster.

Correct check

But the task is not for the person to reach the car wash. The task is to wash the car. And the car must be at the car wash.

You need to check not only the answer, but also what task AI is actually solving.

Weak prompt

"Recommend a good laptop for me."

The problem is that AI does not know the budget, country, tasks, battery requirements, weight, screen, warranty, or tradeoffs.

Better context

"I need a laptop within my budget for programming, browsing, video calls, and working on the road. Battery life, quiet operation, and a good keyboard matter. Give me 3 options and explain the tradeoffs."

Here AI understands not just the product, but the real decision situation.

Weak prompt

"Make me a plan to learn programming."

This kind of prompt almost guarantees a generic plan that sounds fine but is not tied to the person's goal, time, or current level.

Better context

"I want to build a simple web project in 3 months. I can study 1 hour a day and I only know basic computer use. Build a weekly plan with practice, the result of each stage, and what to check."

Now there is a goal, timeline, current level, constraints, and a way to measure progress.

09

Final thought

AI does not replace the path to mastery. It makes that path faster, deeper, and more accessible for people who actually work.

It is not enough to simply know that AI exists. It is not enough to write a prompt and wait for a ready-made solution. You need to learn how to use the tool, understand your field, provide context, build a plan, have goals, verify, doubt, try, and change your approach.

My view today is this: AI can strongly expand what a person can do, but only when the person remains the active side of the work. Not a passive observer. Not someone who simply waits for an answer. A person who searches, thinks, verifies, and moves forward.
